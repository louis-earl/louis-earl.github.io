Project Overview

I was tasked to completely redesign an important PDF report—used by lender managers who need to make quick, high-stakes decisions. The challenge was to turn a complex, cluttered document into a clear and concise summary. The new report had to get straight to the point.

Background

In India, when a lender requests a valuation for a high-risk property, multiple valuation firms are often engaged to assess the same property. Once all valuations are submitted, the Valocity platform compares the data and generates a report—the Executive Summary. This report is reviewed by high-level managers who need to make fast, informed decisions.

My Role

I was the designer for this project, with a focus on layout and information hierarchy. Our marketing team provided the visual standards for print, and I implemented them across the report. I also designed a new in-platform viewer so users could preview the report before downloading the PDF.

Understanding the Problem

The old report was a monster—up to 24 pages long, with key insights buried under layers of scattered content. It was clear why managers struggled: the layout made it hard to scan, and the important stuff just didn't stand out.

To guide the redesign, we gathered insights directly from users. We learned what managers really needed to know—fast:

  • Are the deviations (in values provided by different valuation firms) within an acceptable range?
  • Can I trust the results from each valuation firm?
  • Is it safe for me to approve a loan for this property?

Armed with this real user feedback, I focused on restructuring the report so these critical questions could be answered immediately, right from the first page.

Key Design Decisions

Front Page Summary

The biggest design challenge was making the first page so comprehensive, that managers could make an informed decision without looking any further. It had to act like a dashboard—dense with useful information, but not visually overwhelming. I went through many low-fidelity iterations to find the right balance of density, clarity, and flow.

Reconsidering the Map

Some surprising feedback we had from the old design: lenders wanted the map on the front page. Why? Because lenders in India need to verify that valuers had actually visited the property—not just faked it from their desk. I ensured the map was a prominent feature, with pins that only need to stand out when something's not right.

Flag System

The platform already compared valuers' inputs and flagged discrepancies. I made the flag count prominent on the front page. No flags? All good. But if issues are flagged, the reader knows instantly to take a deeper look at the full report.

New Graph Layout

We knew the old graph didn't include enough data points to support confident decision-making, so the redesigned graph had to cram in more data without overwhelming the reader, or overlapping labels. Through multiple iterations driven by user feedback, I refined the layout to balance data density with clarity—ensuring outliers and patterns pop instantly.

Final Result

I handed off the designs to our India-based dev team, who implemented the report using our PDF generation service. The new in-platform viewer also let users preview reports before downloading.

While the final length varies depending on the job, the redesigned reports now average 10-12 pages—a huge reduction from the original 18-24. More importantly, managers can now make informed decisions faster, with everything they need front and center.
