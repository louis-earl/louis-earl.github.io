Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Research Neuroplasticity

Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people

Neuroplasticity is the ability of the brain to re-wire itself. Without this ability, any brain, not just the human brain, would be unable to develop from infancy through to adulthood or recover from brain injury.

By understanding neuroplasticity, we understand that the malleable structure of our brains can be used to our advantage. We continue to have the ability to learn new activities, skills or languages even into old age. We don’t need to be intelligent to learn - we learn to be intelligent.

Discuss how you might engage with the principals of neuroplasticity for your own benefit

I personally engage with the principals of neuroplasticity as a student of EDA. As I learn JavaScript, I am re-wiring the neural pathways in my brain. The structural and chemical changes happening in my brain allow me to recall knowledge for future use.

As I continue to learn JavaScript (and re-learn CSS and HTML), my brain engages with the same neural pathways again and again. Like widening a busy motorway, my brain strengthens the busy neural neural pathways because they are engaged with repeatedly. So with repeated practice, the end result is a stronger set of skills (memory and problem solving) to work with JS, CSS and HTML.

What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity

Sleep is a vital part of neuroplasticity - The sleep deprived brain is hyperconnected. It’s muddled with connections, dense with nervous information. Sleep restores that. Sleep provides a soft wipe of the brain, giving you the opening necessary to lay down new connections, form new memories, and learn new skills.

Research Growth Mindset

Discuss what Growth Mindset is and why it is relevant

After studying the behaviour of thousands of children, Dr. Dweck coined the terms fixed mindset and growth mindset to describe the underlying beliefs people have about learning and intelligence.

Individuals who believe their talents can be developed (through hard work, good strategies, and input from others) have a growth mindset. They tend to achieve more than those with a more fixed mindset (those who believe their talents are innate gifts). This is because they worry less about looking smart and they put more energy into learning. 

In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?

I personally found the growth mindset compelling - that if you believe your skills and talents can be developed through practice and hard work, you will achieve more.

I admit that I have had a fixed mindset for the most part of my studies (prior to EDA). I’m currently trying to move away from the fixed mindset of being limited to the capacity of my existing genetics and talents, and trying to empower myself with the idea of change - especially that it’s possible to achieve with determination.

How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey?

Firstly, I will try to view all challenges as opportunities for self-improvement. With this, I can drive myself towards change without having to worry about failure.

On that note, all failures are an opportunity to learn. I find the most valuable learnings actually come from when things haven’t been successful - you usually discover something you might not have picked up on otherwise.

Finally, acknowledging and embracing imperfections. No matter how hard I try, I’ll never be perfect. But I won’t be able to overcome my weaknesses without facing them first.
